The situation

We are more complex than our tools suggest

The Lab is not a simple business. We run seven departments under one roof, we work across disciplines that few other companies combine, and we do it at a pace where communication failures have real, immediate consequences.

A client calls a set-build manager with a build job. That job eventually needs studio space, a photographer, catering for a three-day shoot, and a truck to move the set. Another client emails a producer directly — a full production, multiple studios, crew from four departments. A third client contacts rental about equipment they want to hire — which turns into a shoot that Production picks up and Set-build supports.

Jobs enter from every direction. They cross department lines as a matter of course. And most of the coordination that holds it all together happens through email threads, iMessage groups, phone calls, and the collective memory of the people in this building.

That works — until someone is out, something changes at the last minute, or two departments are operating from a different version of the same brief.

We have been very good at making it work anyway. But there is a cost: time spent chasing information that should already be shared, decisions made on incomplete pictures, and the constant low-level friction of running a connected operation through disconnected tools.

The idea

One place where every job lives

The idea is straightforward: a single internal tool built specifically for how The Lab works. Not adapted from a generic project management platform. Not a workaround built on top of something designed for a different kind of company. Built from scratch, for us.

Whatever department a job comes through — whoever takes the call or reads the email — it gets entered into one system. From that point forward, every department that touches the job can see what they need to see: the scope, the timeline, the status, the requests made to them, and what they have committed to deliver.

The goal is not to change how we work. The goal is to give everyone a shared, structured view of what is happening — so that the coordination we already do well becomes faster, clearer, and less dependent on any one person holding all the information in their head.

How we work

Seven departments.
One job.

Every department at The Lab has two sides: the management and planning side, and the execution side. And every department can be the first point of contact for a new job.

This is important because most tools assume a single entry point — a reception desk, a central coordinator, a fixed intake process. We do not work that way, and we should not have to. A tool built for The Lab needs to reflect that a job can begin anywhere and must flow naturally to wherever it needs to go.

The seven departments

Production
Project management and execution
Producers and PMs who handle direct client contact, scope, budget, and planning — and then execute. Full productions, photo shoots, campaigns. Planning and doing are both in this department.
Set-build
Project management and execution
Has its own management layer — set-build managers who handle direct client contact, scoping, estimating, process planning, 3D drawing and rendering, material sourcing, supplier relations, and sub-contractor coordination. Rental / Equipment
Equipment management and hire
Direct client contact for external equipment hire, and internal support for productions happening in the building. Manages inventory, availability, and logistics for everything that goes out and comes back.
Assistants

Photographers' and directors' assistants. Scheduled across shoots, studios, and call times. Their work is defined by other departments' projects — they need a clear, reliable view of their week.

Operates an in-house restaurant and café open to the public for breakfast and lunch — and simultaneously provides catering for every production running in the building. Finance and operations
Financial oversight across all projects and departments. Estimates, costs, invoicing, and the connection to E-conomic. Needs visibility across everything — not involvement in day-to-day, just a clear financial picture.
What it does

A job, from first contact to final invoice

The tool follows the full life of a job. Every stage is connected to the one before and the one after. Nothing is entered twice.

When a job comes in — through any department — it becomes a record. That record carries everything relevant about the job: who the client is, what is being produced, which departments are involved, what has been scoped and estimated, what each department has been asked to deliver, what the status is, and where the money sits.

Each department sees their slice of that record — in a form that makes sense for their work. A set-build manager sees a scope and a budget. A set-build crew member sees their tasks for the week. The kitchen sees headcount by date. Administration sees the financial position across all active jobs.

The same job. Different views. One record.

The full chain

  • Intake — A new enquiry is recorded. Client, rough scope, departments involved, dates in discussion. Not a commitment — just a record that the conversation happened.
  • Scoping — The relevant departments define what they are being asked to do. Scope is structured, not free-text. Studios, crew, equipment, duration — specified, not described.
  • Estimating — Each department that has a cost builds their estimate. The total financial picture is assembled in one place. Production, Set-build, Rental — all contributing to one job estimate.
  • Planning — The job is broken into phases and tasks. Who does what, when, where. Requests go to the departments that need to fulfil them.
  • Execution — Tasks are live. Status updates in real time. Each department works from their own view. Booking can see the full picture. A date change is visible to everyone who needs to know.
  • Wrap-up — The job closes. Actuals are recorded against the estimate. What was planned versus what happened. A reference for next time.
  • Invoicing — The approved estimate becomes the basis for an invoice. Connected to E-conomic. No double-entry.
What it is not

Some things worth saying clearly

This is not an attempt to manage people or measure productivity. There is no time tracking. There is no approval chain that does not already exist in how we work. The tool trusts that people know their jobs.

This is not a replacement for E-conomic. Our financial management stays there. This tool handles the operational picture — scope, planning, status, estimates — and connects to E-conomic at the points where they overlap, to avoid entering the same numbers in two places.

This is not a communication platform. We are not replacing email or iMessage. The tool stores decisions and commitments, not conversations. What was agreed goes in. How you got there stays wherever it happened.

And this is not a system that only works if everyone uses it perfectly from day one. It is designed to be useful even when adoption is partial — to start simple, and expand as it proves its value.

How we build it

The principles behind every decision

A tool like this can go wrong in many ways — too complicated, too generic, too ambitious, or too rigid for the way things actually work here. These principles exist to prevent that.

Any department can start a job
There is no single correct entry point. A job is recorded wherever it is received — and the system routes it from there. No department is a gatekeeper for another.
Entered once, visible everywhere it matters
Information about a job is captured once and shared with every department that needs it. No one re-enters what someone else already provided. The chain from intake to invoice is unbroken.
Each department sees only what is relevant to them
The kitchen does not need to read an estimate. An assistant does not need to see the full process plan. Everyone gets a view that is tailored to their work — clear, not cluttered.
The building is built in
Studios, floors, equipment, and locations are not free-text fields — they are real entities in the system. Studio 3 is Studio 3. The physical reality of the building is part of the data model.
Fast enough for shoot day
The test for every feature: can someone get what they need in ten seconds, on a phone, in a busy studio? If not, it is not done. Speed and clarity are not optional for a physical production environment.
Simple to start, built to grow
We do not need to build everything at once. We start with what delivers the most value immediately and expand deliberately. Each phase is useful on its own — and a foundation for the next.
It reflects how we actually work
No tool survives contact with reality if it was designed for a different reality. Every decision about how this works will be made by people who understand what happens in this building — not by a software company that has never been here.
The roadmap

Built in four phases

The plan is not to build everything at once and hope it works. It is to build in sequence — starting with what is most valuable and most used, and expanding as each layer proves itself.

01
Foundation — jobs, estimates, and process planning
Production / Set-build / Booking

A structured way to take in a job, scope it, estimate it, and plan it. Each job has a status. The pipeline is visible. Planning and cost are connected.

  • Job intake — any department can record a new enquiry or job
  • Project status: Inquiry / Active / In Production / Delivered / Invoiced
  • Estimate — scope and cost, per department, assembled into one job total
  • Process plan — phases, tasks, crew, timeline
  • Pipeline view — all active jobs by status
02
All departments in the system
Kitchen / Rental / Assistants / All departments

Every department has a view that is relevant to their work. Catering orders, equipment requests, assistant schedules — all connected to the jobs they belong to.

  • Catering orders per job: date, location, headcount, meal type
  • Equipment requests: what goes out, when, for which job
  • Assistant schedule: shoot days, studios, call times, kit — per week
  • Department-specific task views — each team sees their own queue
  • Cross-department requests: formal asks between departments, with status
03
Resources — studios, equipment, crew
Booking / Rental / All Team Leaders

The building's resources become bookable inside the system. Studio availability is visible. Equipment inventory is managed. Crew capacity is trackable.

  • Studio calendar: reservations tied to jobs, double-booking prevention
  • Equipment inventory: availability across all active jobs
  • Crew capacity: assigned work versus available headcount, per department
  • Logistics: deliveries, vehicle use, material movements
04
Financial close — actuals, invoicing, and E-conomic
Administration / Production / Set-build

Close the loop between what was planned and what was spent. Connect with E-conomic to reduce double-entry. Give Administration the financial overview they need across all jobs.

  • Actual cost tracking versus estimate — delta visible while the job runs
  • Invoice generation from the approved estimate
  • E-conomic integration — financial data flows, not re-entered
  • Supplier database: external companies, rates, history
  • Job wrap-up: actuals versus planned, notes for next time
  • Financial overview: revenue by job type, margin, utilization

This has been
possible for a while.
We just have not built it.

Every department at The Lab has felt the friction of running a connected operation through disconnected tools. We have managed — because the people here are good at making things work. But there is a better version of this, and it is not complicated to describe.

One place where every job lives. Every department with a view that is useful to them. Information entered once and shared everywhere it is needed. A financial picture that is always current. A system that reflects how this company actually works.

This is a proposal for that system. The plan is to build it in phases, starting with what matters most. The first phase is something we can have running quickly. Each phase after that is a choice we make together based on where the most value is.

The question is not whether this would be useful. The question is whether we want to build it.
